VELO
The Challenge

Finance tools built
for salaries, not
for freelancers.

See our approach

Every mainstream personal finance tool is built around a monthly salary. The math is simple: income comes in on a fixed date, expenses go out, the difference is savings. For freelancers, none of that holds. Income arrives in spikes — a €6,000 project payment one week, nothing for three. Expenses blur between personal and professional. Tax liability compounds invisibly throughout the year and then arrives as a shock.

Existing freelancer tools were built by accountants, not designers. They were ledgers with slightly prettier interfaces — technically capable, emotionally exhausting. User research with 60 freelancers across Germany revealed a consistent pattern: most kept their finances in spreadsheets not because spreadsheets were good, but because existing apps made them feel judged, overwhelmed, or both.

Velo's founding team had deep fintech infrastructure experience but had never shipped a consumer product. They needed a design partner who could translate their technical capability into something a freelance illustrator in Berlin would actually use every day.

Our Approach

Model the reality,
not the
ideal scenario.

The first design principle was radical: stop trying to impose structure onto irregular income, and start designing around it. Velo's financial model treats income as a stream, not a schedule — visualised as a flowing river rather than a bar chart, emphasising velocity and direction over fixed monthly totals.

We built the core UX around three jobs freelancers actually need: knowing whether they can afford to take on new work right now, understanding roughly what their tax liability is at any given moment, and separating business from personal expenses without friction. Every other feature was cut or deferred until those three worked beautifully.

The design system was built for trust — a domain where visual credibility matters enormously. Dark navy palette, precise typography, data visualisations that showed exactly what they claimed to show. We ran usability testing every two weeks throughout the build, with a consistent panel of freelancers who gave us unfiltered feedback. Several became Velo's most vocal early advocates.

The Results

What we
delivered.

Velo launched to a private beta in September 2023. The waitlist — built on nothing but a landing page and word of mouth in freelancer communities — hit 10,000 sign-ups in six weeks. The product had touched a nerve that existing tools had consistently missed.

NPS at three months post-launch was 71, exceptionally high for a fintech product in its first cohort. The most commonly cited reason: "It doesn't make me feel stupid about money." For a product category built around judgment and complexity, that was the signal the team had been designing toward.

The product's design credibility became a commercial asset. Velo's seed deck opened with screenshots of the product. Two of the investors cited the visual quality as a signal of product rigour in their term sheet conversations.

What wasn't working

  • ✕ Calendar-month model assumed fixed salary — meaningless for irregular income
  • ✕ Tax section required accounting knowledge to use correctly
  • ✕ No distinction between personal and business expenses without manual tagging
  • ✕ Data-heavy interface felt intimidating rather than empowering
  • ✕ No emotional tone — the product felt like software, not a financial companion

What we improved

  • ✓ Stream-based income model reflecting how freelance money actually moves
  • ✓ Plain-language tax estimation — no accounting knowledge required
  • ✓ One-tap personal/business expense split trained on freelance patterns
  • ✓ Calm, confident visual language that communicates financial stability
  • ✓ NPS of 71 at 3 months — top quartile for fintech consumer products
